Michigan Buyer Closing Costs: What to Expect

Most of what a Michigan buyer pays at closing is the same as anywhere else — with a couple of state-specific wrinkles worth knowing about.

Closing costs in Michigan generally run in the same 2–3% of the loan amount range you'd see nationally, made up of lender fees, title-related charges, and prepaid amounts. A few things are worth knowing specifically about how Michigan handles them.

The core buyer costs (same as most states)

  • Lender fees — origination, underwriting, processing
  • Appraisal — typically $500–650
  • Title insurance (owner's and lender's policies) — protects against defects in the property's title history
  • Recording fees — paid to the county register of deeds to record the new deed and mortgage
  • Prepaid interest and escrow — the first few months of taxes and insurance, deposited upfront

Michigan's transfer tax — usually a seller cost, but know it exists

Michigan charges a real estate transfer tax when a property changes hands: $3.75 per $500 of value at the state level, plus a county tax of $0.55 per $500 (or $0.75 per $500 in counties that have opted into the higher rate) — combined, typically around $8.60 per $1,000 of sale price. By custom (not law), this is almost always paid by the seller in Michigan, not the buyer. Still, it's worth understanding since it occasionally comes up in negotiation, especially in a slower market where a buyer might ask the seller to cover more of the transaction costs in exchange for a higher price, or vice versa.

Title insurance rates in Michigan

Michigan title insurance premiums are typically filed and fairly consistent between underwriters for a given sale price, unlike some states where you can shop title insurance rates more aggressively. You can still shop which company handles the closing (and their service fees), even if the insurance premium itself doesn't vary much.

What's negotiable

  • Seller concessions — asking the seller to credit some of your closing costs is common and often negotiated as part of the offer, especially when the market favors buyers
  • Which title company/closing agent you use — service fees can vary between companies even if insurance premiums don't
  • Lender fees — origination and processing fees can sometimes be negotiated or offset with a slightly different rate, worth asking your loan officer directly
Don't forget the tax reset: beyond the closing-day costs above, Michigan buyers should also budget for the possibility of a higher ongoing tax bill after your property's taxable value uncaps — see our full guide to Michigan property taxes after a sale.

A realistic total

On a $275,000 Michigan home with 10% down, a typical buyer might see roughly $6,000–7,500 in closing costs (lender, title, appraisal, recording, prepaid interest and escrow combined) — on top of the $27,500 down payment. Frequently asked questions

Does the buyer ever pay Michigan transfer tax?

It's not required by law to be a seller cost — it's just the overwhelmingly common local custom. In an unusual negotiation, it could theoretically be shifted, but assume it's a seller cost unless your purchase agreement says otherwise.

Are Michigan closing costs higher or lower than the national average?

Roughly in line with national averages — Michigan doesn't stand out as unusually expensive or cheap for buyer-side closing costs.

Can I shop for my own title company in Michigan?

Yes, buyers generally have the right to choose their own title company, even though the seller's agent or lender may suggest one. Comparing service fees (not just the insurance premium) is worth doing.
